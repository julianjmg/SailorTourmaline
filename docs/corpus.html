Comparing Radio Plays with a Collection of Suspense Radio Plays

Comparing Word Clusters

Based on the information from Voyant Tools, I found out that the most frequently used words in the Radio Play Collection were: oh, know, yes, sound, and it’s. This made quite a bit of sense for the collection of radio plays because of the large variety, but then I took a look into the suspense plays and I found that the most frequently used words were: sound, oh, music, yes, and I’m, which kind of surprised me. I figured that there would be more “spooky” or suspenseful words, but over half of the most frequently used words are shared with the larger radio play collection. And of course, the words in common were: oh, sound, and yes. That made me wonder how these words are used differently in each collection of radio plays. So, then I began to look at these words in context. First, I’ll be looking at the word sound.

"sound"

I took these files into AntConc and I searched for the word sound in context. Once again, I wasn’t too surprised with what I found for the large collection of radio plays. “Sound” was used for sound effects in the scripts for the radio plays and they mostly contained a lot of applause, laughing, and knocking types of sound effects. But what I did find for the Suspense radio plays was that the sound effects were definitely more specified for this genre. I found a lot of police sirens, pounding, rattling, and radio sound effects, which I found pretty interesting. When I think of the suspense genre, I often think of the corny thriller/suspense movies that would be shown on the lifetime channel, and I think that these sounds are something that I would definitely be hearing in those movies. Next, I’m going to be taking a look at the word “oh” and how it compares in each collection.

"oh"

I know that the word oh is used as a reaction whether that reaction be positive or negative and I think that the large collection of radio plays will use “oh” as a mix of both positive and negative reactions, whereas the suspense collection will use oh in a more negative sense.

I was somewhat right about the suspense radio plays. There was a decent number of negative reactions such as “oh, my arm” and “oh, my clock” but I found something a bit more frequent and interesting. Something that I had completely forgotten about with the word “oh,” was calling out to someone. And this makes complete sense for the suspense genre because someone would be in dire need and they would be calling out “oh John!” or “oh Mrs. Robinson!” for help. This was something that really surprised me as I didn’t even think about the use of “oh” in this type of way.

When I was looking at “oh” in the context of the large collection of radio plays, I found that my guess was pretty accurate. The “oh” was consistently a reaction towards something either positive or negative, but I didn’t see much of the calling out for someone that took place in the suspense plays. My reasoning for this is that it may be more of a genre-specific thing which is why the “calling out for someone” wasn’t seen as frequently in the large collection of radio plays.

Most Frequent N-Grams

minimum size of 2

"don't"

When I first looked at the suspense radio play N-grams, the most frequently used n-gram with a minimum size of 2 was don’t. I immediately wanted to look further into this and I wanted to see if “don’t would appear as frequently as in the large collection of radio plays. And as it turns out, “don’t” appears less frequently in comparison to the suspense radio plays.

I had found that the most frequent N-Gram is "don't" which can be associated with negativity/negation. And I think it's pretty cool how that's the most frequent N-Gram in the suspense radio plays because usually in this type of genre, there's always a character that says "don't do this" and foreshadows future events, usually tragedies. And this type of “forewarning” happened quite a bit. There was a lot of “don’t do it”, “don’t (insert name), and so on. When I looked at the most frequent N-Grams for all of the large collection of radio plays, I found "don't" as the third most frequent N-Grams. But when I took a look at the Key Words in Context, I found that its use was quite a bit different. “Don't” was used in a more conversational way rather than as a warning such as: "don't agree with him" or "don't answer him.”

Most Frequent N-Grams

minimum size of 3

"then in BG"

When I moved to the n-gram size of 3, I found something super cool in the suspense collection! I had discovered the n-gram “THEN IN BG” which I’m assuming means then in background. From what it looks like out of context, it seems that it would be used to set up this super dramatic event. And it turns out that’s exactly what it does. I saw a ton of things like “in the background, David saw something” and even combined “THEN IN BG” with “SOUND” to create an even more suspenseful mood. They were used together to create sounds like police sirens, hurried footsteps, and running steps in snow. Because of how important it seems that “THEN IN BG” seems to be in the suspense genre, it made me wonder how frequently and how differently it could be used in the large collection of radio plays.

In comparison to the suspense plays, the large collection of radio plays used “then in BG” less frequently. The large collection used “then in BG” 183 times and ranked 50 based on frequency while the suspense collection of radio plays used it 162 times and ranked 2 based on frequency. And I think that this is pretty considerable because the large collection has 199 text files while the suspense collection only has 38 total text files. This means that “then in BG” must have been more commonly used in this type of genre then in any other genre in radio plays. Not only that, but “sound” when used in conjunction with “then in BG” was used less frequently in the large collection than the suspense collection. And I think that this was more abundant in the suspense collection because the background sounds would be adding more layers to the suspensefulness of it all making a more engaging environment for their listeners.
